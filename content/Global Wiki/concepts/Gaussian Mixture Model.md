# Gaussian Mixture Model

## Short definition

A Gaussian mixture model (GMM) is a probability distribution formed as a
**weighted sum of several Gaussian distributions**. With enough components it can
approximate almost any complex, even multi-modal density — the continuous
counterpart to a discrete categorical model.

## General explanation

**The building block: the Gaussian.** In one dimension the normal distribution is
fixed by a mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$ (variance $\sigma^2$). In
$n$ dimensions it becomes the **multivariate Gaussian** with mean vector $\mu$
and **covariance matrix** $\Sigma$. The covariance matrix controls the shape: it
is symmetric, its **diagonal** holds the per-dimension variances and its
**off-diagonal** entries the covariances. $\Sigma=I$ gives a circle; a diagonal
matrix with unequal entries stretches along the axes; off-diagonal terms
correlate the dimensions and *rotate* the distribution.

**The mixture.** A GMM combines $M$ Gaussian components with mixing weights
$\pi_m$ — the probability that a data point was generated by component $m$:
$p(x)=\sum_m \pi_m\,\mathcal N(x;\mu_m,\Sigma_m)$ with $\pi_m\ge0$ and
$\sum_m\pi_m=1$. More components mean a finer approximation; one Gaussian bell
can only describe a single hump, but a weighted sum can have many peaks and
arbitrary shapes.

**Two ways to read a GMM.** (1) As **density estimation**: a flexible way to fit
an arbitrary smooth density to data. (2) As a **generative latent-variable
model**: a discrete latent state $C$ (which component) with weights $p(C)=\pi$
and state-conditional Gaussians $p(O\mid C)=\mathcal N(\mu_C,\Sigma_C)$. Reading
it generatively, you sample a component then a point from it; reading it for
inference, you classify a point by the MAP component
$\arg\max_c p(C{=}c\mid O{=}o)=\arg\max_c \pi_c\,\mathcal N(o;\mu_c,\Sigma_c)$.

**Fitting.** Because the component labels are latent, GMMs are usually fit by the
**Expectation–Maximisation (EM)** algorithm, alternating soft assignment of
points to components (E-step) with re-estimation of the weights, means and
covariances (M-step). EM is itself a form of maximum-likelihood estimation
([[Maximum Likelihood Estimation]]).

## Intuition

One Gaussian bell describes a single hill. The real world often has several
clusters — pixels belong to "wall," "bush," "sign," or "road," each with its own
typical brightness. Throw a bell at each cluster and **add them up, weighted**:
the total curve can now have several peaks and arbitrary shape. You build a
complex distribution out of simple parts, like mixing any melody from a few base
tones.

## Formal definition

1-D Gaussian:
$\mathcal N(x;\mu,\sigma)=\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\exp\!\big(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\big)$.
Multivariate:

$$
\mathcal N(x;\mu,\Sigma)=\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{n/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}}
\exp\!\Big(-\tfrac12(x-\mu)^{\top}\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu)\Big).
$$

Mixture: $p(x)=\sum_{m=1}^{M}\pi_m\,\mathcal N(x;\mu_m,\Sigma_m)$. Symbols:
$\mu$ mean (vector), $\Sigma$ covariance matrix, $|\Sigma|$ its determinant,
$\pi_m$ mixing weight, $n$ dimensions, $M$ number of components.

## Worked example

Two classes, brightness $o\in[0,255]$: "road" $\sim\mathcal N(80,20)$, "wall"
$\sim\mathcal N(180,25)$, priors $0.6$ and $0.4$. For a pixel with $o=150$: the
road bell has already fallen off steeply ($3.5\sigma$ away) so
$p(o\mid\text{road})\,p(\text{road})$ is tiny, while the wall term ($1.2\sigma$
below $180$) is much larger ⇒ MAP decision **wall**. The overall density of all
pixels is the two-peaked mixture
$p(o)=0.6\,\mathcal N(80,20)+0.4\,\mathcal N(180,25)$.

## Why it matters

GMMs are a workhorse for clustering, density estimation, soft classification, and
as a building block in larger probabilistic and generative models. They are the
standard concrete example of a continuous latent-variable model and of the EM
algorithm.

## Common confusions

- **Covariance ≠ just spread.** The diagonal is spread (variance); only the
  off-diagonal encodes dependence/rotation. $\Sigma=I$ ⇒ uncorrelated, round.
- **Mixing weights are probabilities:** $\pi_m\ge0$, $\sum_m\pi_m=1$ — not
  arbitrary scaling factors.
- **Sampling ≠ structure:** sampling pixels independently from $p(o\mid c)$
  reproduces the right marginal statistics but no coherent spatial structure.
