# Predictive Coding

Predictive coding is a theory about how your brain makes sense of the world. Instead of just sitting back and waiting for information to come in through your eyes and ears, your brain is constantly **guessing** what’s about to happen next.

Think of your brain as a "prediction engine" rather than a passive camera.
### How It Works
The process is a constant loop between two things: **Predictions** and **Errors**.

1. **Top-Down Predictions:** Your brain uses past experiences to build a model of the world. It sends signals "down" to your senses telling them what it expects to see, hear, or feel.
    
2. **Bottom-Up Errors:** Your senses take in information from the real world. If the world matches your prediction, your brain stays quiet. If there is a mismatch, it generates a **Prediction Error**.
    
3. **The Update:** When an error occurs, the signal travels "up" to the brain, forcing it to update its internal model so the next guess is more accurate.
---
### A Real-World Example: The Stairs

Imagine you are walking down a flight of stairs in the dark.

- **The Prediction:** Your brain "predicts" there is one more step. It prepares your muscles and balance for that specific drop.
    
- **The Error:** If you’ve actually reached the floor, your foot hits the ground sooner than expected.
    
- **The Result:** You feel a "jolt" of surprise. That jolt is a massive **prediction error signal**. Your brain instantly updates: "We are on flat ground now," and you stop trying to step down.

### Why Does the Brain Do This?

It’s all about **efficiency**. Processing every single detail of your surroundings from scratch every second would take an enormous amount of energy. By only focusing on _changes_ or _surprises_ (the errors), your brain saves power and reacts much faster to the environment.

---

### Key Takeaway

In predictive coding, **perception is just a "controlled hallucination."** You aren't seeing the world exactly as it is; you are seeing your brain’s best guess of the world, updated by occasional corrections from your senses.
