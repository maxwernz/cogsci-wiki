# Wichmann & Hill (2001a) — The Psychometric Function: I. Fitting, Sampling, and Goodness of Fit

> Cited in [[VL04 Assessing variability]] / the [[ReadingList-VL2-5.pdf|reading list]] as the **optional companion** to the compulsory [[Wichmann and Hill (2001b)]]. Not compulsory — suggested only for those who want to see a complete
> modeling "pipeline" across VL2–VL4.

## Summary

The first of two companion papers that together walk through the *entire*
three-step modeling recipe on one example, the **psychometric function** (how an
observer's performance depends on stimulus intensity). This paper covers **step 1
(parameter estimation/fitting)** and **step 3 (goodness-of-fit)**; the companion
2001b covers **step 2 (confidence intervals via the bootstrap)**. The paper
itself opens by stating the recipe almost verbatim: "Modeling data is a
three-step process… choose a model and adjust parameters to minimise a loss
function; derive error estimates of the parameters; assess goodness of fit."

## Key points

- The psychometric function is a **descriptive** model (it summarises data
  compactly), not a true cognitive/mechanistic model — so do not over-interpret
  its parameters.
- Fitting: maximum-likelihood estimation of the function's parameters (threshold,
  slope, guess rate, lapse rate).
- Goodness-of-fit: assessed with simulations, addressing known pitfalls (e.g.
  the influence of **lapses** at high intensities on threshold/slope estimates).
- Read alongside 2001b to see how parameter estimation, GoF, and confidence
  intervals fit together as one coherent pipeline.

## Local relevance

Provides the "complete pipeline" view tying VL2 (fitting), VL3 (GoF), and VL4
(CIs) together on a single running example. Optional, but the clearest single
demonstration that the three lectures are *one* procedure ([[The Three-Step Recipe for Modeling Data]]).

## Exam or project relevance

Low/optional. The exam-relevant content is the *recipe structure* (which this
paper exemplifies) and, from the compulsory 2001b, the bootstrap and the
features-vs-bugs of confidence intervals.
